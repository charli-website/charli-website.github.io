Datasets - Human Security

 
As part of NSF 0729279, we aimed to study the salience of issues among the organizations most central to the human security network. We operationalized the network by using the Issuecrawler co-link analysis tool, with the top 10 Google search results for “Human Security” as starting points. We triangulated this with responses to the question “Which organizations come to mind when you think of human security” on a web survey disseminated using a snowball method that began with the Liu Institute of Global Issues’s mailing list. The organizations in the network that results are listed in the XLS file below; visualizations of the online linked network can be found on the Issuecrawler site; raw data on the triangulated network is here.

We then examined the website of each of 49 organizations at the core of the network and captured the “Mission Statement” and “Issue List” for each. This raw text data was then uploaded in to Atlas.ti 6.0 and annotated by independent coders associated with the Qualitative Data Analysis Program. These web indicators of issue salience were triangulated with additional survey answers to the question “Which issues come to mind when you think of human security today?” Survey and focus group answers were summarized, compared to and triangulated with the web data. We also collected information on issues that are not currently salient on the human security agenda, to understand how these might be different from those that do get attention. Survey respondents and focus group participants were asked to name “human security problems can you think of that are not very prominent as issues in the human security movement?” We dropped any issues that were actually present in the human security network as determined by the content analysis, leaving a bank of issues mentioned that were not salient on the network agenda at the time the data were gathered.

Focus groups were used to refine hypotheses about variation in the human security issue agenda and to gather data on network practitioners’ reactions when asked to consider specific low-salience issues in a conversational setting. Participants were recruited from organizations identified as being the network, with efforts made to create thematic and organizational diversity within each thematic group. 43 individuals participated in six focus groups in fall 2009 at the Fletcher School of Law and Diplomacy in Medford, MA.

Focus group transcripts are currently being coded and analyzed. Findings from the focus groups will be detailed in a forthcoming report, and published by Cornell University Press as Setting the Advocacy Agenda: The Politics of Issue Creation in Advocacy Networks. Descriptive statistics from the project will be available in Fall 2010 and replication data will be available after publication.
