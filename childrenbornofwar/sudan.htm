Case Study: Darfur
Robyn Wheeler


What is the background to the conflict?
The ongoing conflict in the Darfur region of the Sudan continues to result in births as a result of mass rape and sexual slavery. A long history of discontent was pushed to major significance in 2003 when two rebel groups representing the non-Arabs citizens of the region began to attack government installations. The government swiftly empowered the Janjaweed, a militia comprised of local Arabs, to strike back on their behalf, and the war began. Hundreds of thousands of Sudanese have been affected by this conflict either through famine, displacement, murder or rape.

What is the nature and extent of the sexual violence?
As in many international conflict settings, sexual violence including gang rape and sexual slavery has been used in Darfur as a means to terrorize the civilian population. The Janjaweed, who are documented as using rape as one of their primary methods of attack, rely on the havoc they create and the psychological long-term effects they are fostering.   As they are conservative Muslims themselves, they are fully aware of the resulting stigma and destruction that their sexual violence causes: indeed, there is evidence that this is the primary motivation for these campaigns of mass rape. One mother remembers being told during her rape, “We want to change the color of your children,” which indicates forced impregnation as a further tool of genocide (Raghavan, 2004).

How many children as estimated born as a result?
Specific statistics do not exist tracking the number of babies born from rape during this conflict, but speculation is that thousands of rapes have occurred since the conflict began.  The difficulty with assessing specific numbers lies with the conservative nature of society.  Rape is a taboo that is not to be talked about and consequently the majority of rapes go unreported.  This is coupled with the Sudanese government’s track record of non-punishment of the perpetrators of sexual crimes.

What is the situation of the children and their mothers?
Rape victims in Darfur are often blamed for the violence and it is believed in many villages that it is impossible to become impregnated from unwanted sex. The women and men in Darfur society are culturally raised to believe that rape is avoidable and that the sexual violence the woman has endured should have been prevented.  The women often feel displaced from their community as a result of their ordeal and end up ostracized either by the community or their own shame.  A child born as the result of such violence in most cases ends the mother’s future.  She is no longer marriageable because there is a cultural imperative in this society that a bride be a virgin.  As a result, the victim has cast shame on her entire family by being raped and they will likely react by ostracizing her and the baby. Therefore, if a pregnancy results from rape the mother and child carry a stigma with them for the remainder of their lives. Little research is available to date regarding the infants, possibly because the conflict is still relatively recent and babies are just now being born.  Long-term assessment is required, but initial research indicates the babies are heavily stigmatized and rarely are accepted by the community or in some cases even the mother. Evidence suggests that even where communities accept the babies, they will still be observed for ill temperament and potentially abandoned as a result in the future. According to press reports, such children have been referred to as “Janjaweed babies” or “dirty babies” by the communities into which they are born. Aid workers have reported rumors of infanticides, but these reports have not been independently verified.

What has been the humanitarian response?
UNICEF asserted in February 2005 that “dozens of babies are being born in Darfur to mothers raped during the ongoing conflict in western Sudan.” This organization is one of the only organizations discovered through research that is actively promoting attention be paid to the new generation of babies that have been born as a result of the systematic rape of women in Darfur.  They recommend diffusing the stigma through education of the community, believing that once the members of the community realize the rape was not the mother’s fault and that an innocent child cannot be held accountable for their father’s actions, the stigma will disappear and the mother and child will receive the support they need. In the meantime they emphasize the need for humanitarian organizations to focus on making sure this population is not discriminated against in refugee camps and that they have equal access to health care and education.
